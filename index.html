Phases of the Project Life Cycle

A Project Life Cycle (PLC) is the sequence of phases a project passes through from start to finish. It gives structure, clarity, and direction to the project team.

"A project life cycle is the series of phases that a project passes through from its initiation to its closure." — PMBOK Guide

Phase 1 — Initiation

Project is formally defined and authorized. Key outputs: Project Charter, feasibility study, stakeholder identification. Core question: "Is this worth doing?"

Phase 2 — Planning

Detailed planning of scope, schedule, cost, quality, and risk. Outputs: WBS, Gantt chart, Risk Register, Resource Plan. Most critical phase — errors here cascade everywhere.

Phase 3 — Execution

Actual work of producing deliverables. Teams are directed, resources managed, and stakeholder communication maintained. Consumes the most time and budget.

Phase 4 — Monitoring & Controlling

Runs concurrently with all phases. Tracks performance using SPI, CPI. Manages scope creep, schedule deviations, and budget overruns through change control.

Phase 5 — Closure

Formal acceptance of deliverables, releasing resources, documenting lessons learned, archiving records, and final handover to the client or operations team.

📌
Exam Tip: Monitoring & Controlling is NOT a sequential phase — it runs concurrently with all other phases. Many students lose marks for this mistake.

Project Metrics & Project Evaluation

Project Metrics are quantitative measures used to evaluate, compare, and track project performance. They convert subjective observations into objective, measurable data.

"Project metrics are standardized methods of measurement used to determine the status of a project and detect deviations from the plan."
01 — Type

Process Metrics

Measure quality and efficiency of the development process. E.g., defect removal efficiency, review coverage rate.

02 — Type

Product Metrics

Measure attributes of the software itself. E.g., lines of code (LOC), function points, cyclomatic complexity.

03 — Type

Project Metrics

Measure project-level characteristics. E.g., effort, cost, schedule variance, team size.

04 — Type

People Metrics

Measure team and human factors. E.g., productivity per developer, team turnover rate, skill gaps.

How Metrics Help in Project Evaluation

Early Warning System

SPI and CPI alert managers to deviations before they become critical failures.

Objective Decisions

Replace gut feelings with data-driven decisions on resources, timelines, and scope.

Benchmarking

Compare performance against historical baselines and industry standards.

Stakeholder Reporting

Provide clear, quantifiable progress updates to clients and management.

Process Improvement

Historical data identifies weak areas, driving continuous improvement (CMMI, ISO).

Risk Identification

Negative metric trends signal growing project risk in real-time.

📌
Exam Tip: Remember GQM — Goal → Question → Metric. Define the goal, ask how to measure it, then pick the metric. Very commonly asked in exams.

Common Sources of Risk in IT Projects

A risk is any uncertain event that, if it occurs, has a positive or negative effect on project objectives. Managing risk is one of the core responsibilities of a project manager.

"A risk is an uncertain event or condition that, if it occurs, has an effect on at least one project objective." — PMBOK Guide
Known Risks — Can be planned for Unknown Risks — Cannot be anticipated
HIGH

1. Requirements Risk

Incomplete, ambiguous, or changing requirements lead to scope creep, rework, and missed deadlines. #1 cause of IT project failure.

HIGH

2. Technology Risk

Unproven or evolving technologies, hardware failures, software incompatibilities, and complex integrations.

HIGH

3. People Risk (HR Risk)

Key team members leaving, lack of skilled personnel, poor team communication, or low morale.

MED

4. Estimation Risk

Inaccurate effort, cost, or schedule estimates. Underestimation → budget overruns; overestimation → wasted resources.

HIGH

5. Organizational Risk

Lack of management support, political conflicts, poorly defined roles, and inadequate resource commitment.

MED

6. External / Environmental Risk

Regulatory changes, market shifts, vendor failures, economic conditions, or third-party dependencies.

MED

7. Security / Cybersecurity Risk

Data breaches, unauthorized access, ransomware, and non-compliance with data protection laws (GDPR, IT Act).

LOW

8. Communication Risk

Poor stakeholder communication leads to misunderstood requirements, missed decisions, and delayed approvals.

📌
Exam Tip: Risk responses — Avoid (eliminate), Transfer (insurance/outsource), Mitigate (reduce impact), Accept (acknowledge). Mnemonic: ATMA.

Project Communication Plan

A Project Communication Plan is a formal document defining how, when, and to whom project information will be communicated. Studies show that 80% of a project manager's time is spent on communication.

"A Communication Plan outlines what information needs to be communicated, to whom, how, when, and by whom — ensuring all stakeholders stay informed and aligned."

Key Components

Stakeholder Identification

Who receives information? Clients, teams, sponsors, vendors — each group has different needs.

Information Requirements

What is shared? Status reports, risk logs, budget updates, milestone achievements, issue logs.

Communication Methods

How? Email, meetings, dashboards, Slack, formal reports, SharePoint, presentations.

Frequency & Schedule

When? Daily standups, weekly status, monthly steering meetings, per-milestone reviews.

Responsibility Matrix

Who sends each communication? Clear ownership avoids duplication and gaps.

Escalation Procedures

How are critical issues escalated? Define thresholds, paths, and response timeframes.

Types of Communication

Formal Written — Reports, Documents Formal Verbal — Presentations, Reviews Informal Written — Emails, Chats Informal Verbal — Conversations, Calls

Communication Channels Formula

Channels = n(n − 1) / 2   [where n = number of stakeholders]

Example: 5 stakeholders → 5 × 4 / 2 = 10 channels
Add 1 more person → 6 × 5 / 2 = 15 channels

This shows why communication complexity grows rapidly as team size increases.

📌
Exam Tip: The formula n(n−1)/2 is almost always asked. Also: the communication plan is part of the Planning process group in PMBOK — created during planning and updated throughout.
