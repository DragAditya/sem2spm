Question 01 · CO1 · Understand (BT2)

Explain the Different Phases of the Project Life Cycle.

Project Life CycleCO1BT2
◆Definition
A Project Life Cycle (PLC) is the series of phases that a project passes through from its initiation to its closure. It provides a structured framework that guides the project team from the beginning to the end, ensuring that all deliverables are produced and objectives are met systematically.
◆Phases of Project Life Cycle
Phase 1 — Initiation / Concept Phase

This is the starting phase where the project idea is born. The project's feasibility is evaluated, objectives are defined, and a decision is made whether to proceed. A Project Charter is created authorising the project.

  • Define project purpose, goals, and objectives
  • Identify key stakeholders and project sponsor
  • Conduct feasibility study (technical, financial, operational)
  • Create the Project Charter document
  • Obtain approval to proceed
Phase 2 — Planning Phase

The most critical phase of the project life cycle. Detailed plans are created covering scope, schedule, budget, quality, communication, risk, and resources. The project plan serves as the roadmap for execution.

  • Define project scope and Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)
  • Develop project schedule using Gantt charts, CPM, PERT
  • Estimate costs and develop project budget
  • Identify and plan for project risks
  • Create communication and stakeholder management plans
  • Resource allocation and procurement planning
Phase 3 — Execution Phase

The doing phase — actual project work is carried out. The project manager coordinates resources, manages the team, and ensures work is done according to the project plan. This is where the product or service is built.

  • Assign tasks and manage the project team
  • Execute activities as per the project schedule
  • Manage quality assurance processes
  • Distribute information to stakeholders
  • Manage vendor and procurement activities
Phase 4 — Monitoring & Controlling Phase

This phase runs parallel to execution. The project manager tracks, reviews, and regulates the progress and performance of the project. Changes are managed and corrective actions are taken when needed.

  • Track project performance against baselines (scope, time, cost)
  • Manage changes through a formal change control process
  • Report project status to stakeholders
  • Identify and address deviations from the plan
  • Monitor and control risks
Phase 5 — Closure Phase

The final phase where the project is formally completed and handed over. All activities are wrapped up, lessons learned are documented, and team members are released.

  • Obtain formal acceptance from the customer/client
  • Archive all project documents
  • Conduct post-project review and lessons learned session
  • Release project resources and close contracts
  • Celebrate project success with the team

Question 02 · CO1 · Understand (BT2)

Explain the Role of Project Manager in Detail.

Project ManagerCO1BT2
◆Who is a Project Manager?
A Project Manager (PM) is the person responsible for leading a project from its initiation to closure. The PM is accountable for the entire project scope, team management, stakeholder communication, risk management, and delivery of the project within time, budget, and quality constraints.
◆Key Roles & Responsibilities
1. Project Planning & Scope Management

The PM defines the project scope, creates the Work Breakdown Structure (WBS), and develops detailed project plans including schedules, budgets, and resource plans. They ensure the project objectives are clearly defined and understood.

2. Team Leadership & Management

The PM builds, motivates, and manages the project team. They assign roles and responsibilities, resolve team conflicts, conduct performance reviews, and ensure a productive working environment. Strong interpersonal skills are essential here.

3. Risk Management

The PM identifies, assesses, and mitigates risks throughout the project. They maintain a risk register, develop contingency plans, and regularly monitor potential threats to project success.

4. Stakeholder Communication

The PM acts as the primary communicator between the project team and all stakeholders. They produce regular status reports, conduct meetings, manage expectations, and ensure stakeholders are informed and engaged at all times.

5. Budget & Cost Management

The PM estimates project costs, develops the budget, and controls expenditures throughout the project lifecycle. They use earned value management (EVM) techniques to track financial performance.

6. Quality Management

The PM ensures the project meets defined quality standards. They establish quality assurance processes, conduct reviews, manage testing activities, and ensure deliverables meet specifications.

7. Change Management

The PM manages the change control process, evaluating all proposed changes for their impact on scope, schedule, cost, and quality before approving or rejecting them.

8. Problem Solving & Decision Making

The PM resolves issues that arise during the project. They make timely decisions under pressure, escalate issues when appropriate, and keep the project moving forward.

Skills Required for a Project Manager

▸ Technical Skills — understanding of project domain and tools

▸ Leadership — inspiring and guiding the team toward goals

▸ Communication — clear and effective verbal/written communication

▸ Negotiation — balancing competing stakeholder interests

▸ Risk Awareness — anticipating and preparing for potential issues

▸ Time Management — prioritising tasks and meeting deadlines

Question 03 · CO1 · Understand (BT2)

Explain the Concept of Project Management and Its Importance in Software Projects.

Project ManagementCO1
◆What is Project Management?
Project Management is the application of knowledge, skills, tools, and techniques to project activities in order to meet the project requirements. It involves planning, organising, directing, and controlling all aspects of a project to achieve its objectives within defined constraints of time, cost, and quality.
◆Core Knowledge Areas (PMBOK)
Area 1

Scope Mgmt

Define and control what is and isn't included in the project.

Area 2

Time Mgmt

Plan and control the schedule to deliver on time.

Area 3

Cost Mgmt

Estimate, budget, and control project finances.

Area 4

Quality Mgmt

Ensure deliverables meet defined quality standards.

Area 5

Risk Mgmt

Identify and respond to project uncertainties.

Area 6

Communication

Plan and manage all stakeholder communications.

◆Importance of Project Management in Software Projects
1. Clear Goal Definition

PM ensures that software requirements are clearly understood and documented. Without proper management, software projects often suffer from scope creep — the uncontrolled expansion of project scope.

2. Efficient Resource Utilisation

Software projects involve expensive human resources — developers, testers, analysts. PM ensures optimal allocation of these resources, reducing idle time and preventing burnout.

3. On-Time Delivery

Through careful scheduling and monitoring, PM helps ensure that the software is delivered on time. Late delivery is one of the most common failures in software projects.

4. Cost Control

Software projects frequently go over budget. PM techniques help track expenditures against baselines and take corrective actions before costs spiral out of control.

5. Risk Mitigation

Software development is inherently risky — changing requirements, technology failures, key person dependencies. PM provides a structured approach to identify and mitigate these risks before they become problems.

6. Quality Assurance

PM establishes quality standards and processes to ensure that the software meets user expectations and industry standards, reducing rework and defects.

Triple Constraint (Iron Triangle)

▸ Scope — What must be delivered?

▸ Time — When must it be delivered?

▸ Cost — How much can be spent?

▸ Changes to any one constraint affect the others — PM balances all three.

Question 04 · CO1 · Understand (BT2)

Explain the Concept of Stakeholder Management in Project Management.

StakeholdersCO1
◆Who are Stakeholders?
A stakeholder is any individual, group, or organisation who may affect, be affected by, or perceive themselves to be affected by a decision, activity, or outcome of a project. Stakeholder management is the process of identifying stakeholders, understanding their interests and influence, and developing strategies to engage them effectively.
◆Types of Stakeholders

Internal Stakeholders

Project team members, project manager, project sponsor, senior management, functional managers within the organisation.

External Stakeholders

Customers/clients, end users, suppliers, vendors, regulatory bodies, competitors, the general public.

◆Stakeholder Management Process
Step 1 — Identify Stakeholders

The first step is to identify all individuals and groups who have an interest in or will be affected by the project. A Stakeholder Register is created containing their names, roles, interests, and influence level.

Step 2 — Analyse Stakeholders

Each stakeholder is assessed for their power/interest in the project. A Power-Interest Grid is commonly used — high power, high interest stakeholders are managed most closely.

PowerInterestStrategyExample
HighHighManage CloselyProject Sponsor
HighLowKeep SatisfiedSenior Management
LowHighKeep InformedEnd Users
LowLowMonitorGeneral Public
Step 3 — Plan Stakeholder Engagement

Develop strategies for how to communicate and engage with each stakeholder group. Define frequency, format, and channels of communication. Address concerns and manage expectations proactively.

Step 4 — Manage Engagement

Actively communicate, involve, and respond to stakeholders throughout the project. Hold regular meetings, distribute progress reports, and handle feedback and conflict resolution professionally.

Step 5 — Monitor Stakeholder Engagement

Continuously track stakeholder engagement levels and update strategies as the project progresses and as stakeholder needs change.

💡

Why is Stakeholder Management Important? Unmanaged stakeholders can derail a project. A dissatisfied sponsor may withdraw funding; unhappy users may reject the final product; regulatory bodies may halt delivery. Effective stakeholder management ensures project success.

Question 06 · CO1 · Understand (BT2)

Explain the System View of Project Management in the Context of IT Projects.

System ViewIT ProjectsCO1
◆What is the Systems Approach?
A systems approach to project management views a project not as an isolated activity but as a complex system with multiple interrelated parts that must work together to achieve project goals. It considers the organisation, project environment, people, processes, and technology as a whole system.
◆Systems Thinking in Project Management

Systems thinking encourages project managers to think holistically rather than focusing on individual components. A change in one part of the system affects other parts. For example, reducing the project timeline (time) may require more resources (cost) or reduced scope.

◆Three-Sphere Model of Systems Management
Sphere 1

Business Sphere

Focuses on organisational goals, return on investment, strategic alignment, and business value of the project.

Sphere 2

Technology Sphere

Covers technical aspects — software architecture, tools, platforms, infrastructure, and technical feasibility.

Sphere 3

Organisational Sphere

Addresses people, processes, culture, politics, and organisational structure that influence the project.

◆Systems View Applied to IT Projects
Inputs

IT projects take in various inputs — requirements, resources (people, hardware, software), budget, time, and stakeholder expectations. These are the raw materials of the system.

Processes

The project management processes (planning, executing, monitoring, controlling) transform inputs into outputs. These processes must be efficient and well-coordinated.

Outputs

The outputs are the working software, system documentation, test reports, and trained users. These outputs must meet the requirements defined at the project outset.

Feedback Loops

A key aspect of systems thinking is feedback loops. Monitoring and controlling phases provide continuous feedback that allows the project manager to adjust plans and take corrective action.

📌

Example: In an IT project to build a hospital management system, the systems view considers: business needs (efficient patient records), technology (database, web app), and organisational aspects (training staff, change management) — all as an integrated system.

Question 07 · CO1 · Understand (BT2)

Explain the Context of Information Technology Projects in Project Management.

IT ContextCO1
◆What are IT Projects?
An IT Project is a temporary endeavour undertaken to create a unique IT product, service, or result. Examples include software development, network infrastructure setup, ERP implementation, website development, and cybersecurity system deployment. IT projects have unique characteristics that make them both challenging and exciting.
◆Unique Characteristics of IT Projects
1. Rapidly Changing Technology

Technology evolves at an unprecedented pace. IT projects often face technology obsolescence risks — a platform chosen at project start may be outdated by delivery. PMs must account for technology change throughout the project.

2. Invisible Products

Unlike construction projects where progress is visible (walls, floors), IT project progress is largely invisible. Software is intangible, making it harder for stakeholders to gauge progress. Regular demos and prototypes help address this.

3. High Rate of Failure

Studies (Standish Group CHAOS Report) show that a large percentage of IT projects fail to be delivered on time, on budget, or with the required features. Effective PM reduces this risk significantly.

4. Scope Creep

Scope creep — the continuous, uncontrolled addition of new features — is extremely common in IT projects. As users interact with the system, they request more features. PMs must rigorously manage scope through change control.

5. Team Diversity & Distribution

IT teams are often globally distributed, spanning different time zones, cultures, and languages. Managing virtual teams requires special communication and collaboration skills and tools.

6. Dependency on Human Capital

IT projects are highly people-dependent. The loss of a key developer or architect can severely impact the project. Knowledge management and documentation are critical.

Question 08 · CO2 · Understand (BT2)

What are Project Metrics? How Do They Help in Project Evaluation?

MetricsEvaluationCO2
◆What are Project Metrics?
Project Metrics are quantitative measurements used to assess the performance, progress, and health of a project. They provide objective data that helps project managers and stakeholders make informed decisions, identify problems early, and evaluate project success against defined targets.
◆Categories of Project Metrics
1. Schedule Metrics

Measure whether the project is on track with the planned timeline.

  • Schedule Variance (SV) = Earned Value (EV) − Planned Value (PV). Negative = behind schedule.
  • Schedule Performance Index (SPI) = EV / PV. SPI < 1 means behind schedule.
  • Milestone Completion Rate — percentage of milestones achieved on time
2. Cost Metrics

Measure financial performance of the project.

  • Cost Variance (CV) = Earned Value (EV) − Actual Cost (AC). Negative = over budget.
  • Cost Performance Index (CPI) = EV / AC. CPI < 1 means over budget.
  • Budget Utilisation Rate — actual spend as a percentage of planned spend
3. Quality Metrics

Measure the quality of deliverables produced.

  • Defect Density — number of defects per KLOC (thousand lines of code)
  • Defect Detection Rate — percentage of defects found in testing vs. production
  • Customer Satisfaction Score — measured through surveys and reviews
4. Productivity Metrics

Measure team output and efficiency.

  • Velocity (Agile) — story points completed per sprint
  • Function Points — measure of functional size of software
  • Lines of Code (LOC) — amount of code produced per developer
◆How Metrics Help in Project Evaluation
MetricFormulaInterpretation
SV (Schedule Variance)EV − PVNegative = behind; Positive = ahead
CV (Cost Variance)EV − ACNegative = over budget; Positive = under budget
SPI (Schedule Perf. Index)EV / PV<1 behind, =1 on track, >1 ahead
CPI (Cost Perf. Index)EV / AC<1 over budget, =1 on budget, >1 under

📊

Earned Value Management (EVM) combines scope, schedule, and cost metrics into a single integrated system. It is the most powerful method for project performance measurement and forecasting.

Question 09 · CO2 · Understand (BT2)

Explain Project Schedule in Detail.

ScheduleGanttCPMCO2
◆What is a Project Schedule?
A Project Schedule is a document that defines when project activities will start, how long they will take, and when they will finish. It serves as the baseline for monitoring progress and managing time. It maps every task, dependency, resource, and milestone across the project timeline.
◆Components of a Project Schedule
  • Activities / Tasks — specific work items that must be completed
  • Milestones — significant events or checkpoints in the project
  • Dependencies — relationships between tasks (finish-to-start, start-to-start, etc.)
  • Duration — estimated time needed to complete each activity
  • Resources — people, equipment, and materials assigned to tasks
  • Start & Finish Dates — planned beginning and end of each activity
  • Critical Path — the longest sequence of dependent tasks determining minimum project duration
◆Scheduling Techniques
1. Gantt Chart

A Gantt Chart is a bar chart that shows project activities against a calendar. Each task is represented as a horizontal bar. Its length represents the task duration. Gantt charts provide a visual, easy-to-understand schedule view.

Advantages: Simple, visual, easy to update, shows task overlaps and sequence clearly.
Limitation: Does not easily show dependencies or the critical path for complex projects.
2. Critical Path Method (CPM)

CPM identifies the longest path of dependent tasks through the project network — the critical path. Any delay on the critical path directly delays the entire project. Tasks not on the critical path have float/slack — they can be delayed without impacting the final deadline.

3. PERT (Program Evaluation and Review Technique)

PERT uses probabilistic time estimates for activities:

PERT Expected Time = (Optimistic + 4×Most Likely + Pessimistic) / 6
This accounts for uncertainty in duration estimates, making PERT especially useful for research and development projects.
4. Precedence Diagramming Method (PDM)

PDM represents activities as nodes and dependencies as arrows. Supports four types of dependencies: Finish-to-Start, Start-to-Start, Finish-to-Finish, Start-to-Finish.

◆Schedule Baseline

Once approved, the project schedule becomes the schedule baseline — the agreed reference point against which actual progress is measured throughout the project. Changes require formal change control.

Question 11 · CO2 · Understand (BT2)

Discuss the Importance of Monitoring and Controlling a Project.

MonitoringControllingCO2
◆What is Monitoring & Controlling?
Monitoring and Controlling is the process of tracking, reviewing, and regulating the progress and performance of the project against the project management plan. It occurs throughout the project lifecycle, running parallel to execution. Without monitoring, a project can silently drift off-course before anyone notices.
◆Key Activities in Monitoring & Controlling
  • Performance Reporting — comparing actual vs. planned scope, time, and cost
  • Change Control — evaluating and managing all requested changes
  • Risk Monitoring — tracking identified risks and watching for new ones
  • Quality Control — inspecting deliverables to ensure they meet standards
  • Schedule Control — managing changes to the project schedule baseline
  • Cost Control — managing changes to the project budget baseline
◆Importance of Monitoring & Controlling
1. Early Detection of Problems

Regular monitoring allows the PM to identify deviations from the plan early, before they become critical. A small schedule delay caught early can often be recovered; the same delay caught late may cascade into a major project crisis.

2. Ensures On-Time Delivery

By continuously comparing actual progress against the schedule, the PM can take corrective actions (crashing, fast-tracking) to bring the project back on track.

3. Cost Control

Budget overruns are a leading cause of project failure. Regular cost monitoring using EVM techniques allows the PM to forecast final costs and intervene before spending gets out of control.

4. Stakeholder Confidence

Regular, transparent reporting builds stakeholder trust and confidence. Stakeholders who are kept informed are less likely to interfere unpredictably or withdraw support.

5. Scope Management

Monitoring prevents scope creep — the unauthorised addition of new requirements. Change requests are formally evaluated and only approved when justified, keeping the project focused.

6. Quality Assurance

Controlling quality throughout the project (not just at the end) ensures that defects are caught early, when they are cheapest to fix. The cost of fixing a defect exponentially increases the later it is found.

⚡

Key Principle: "You cannot manage what you cannot measure." Monitoring provides the data; controlling provides the response. Together, they keep the project on its intended course.

Question 12 · CO2 · Understand (BT2)

Describe Information Distribution with Example.

Information DistributionCO2
◆What is Information Distribution?
Information Distribution is the process of making relevant project information available to project stakeholders in a timely manner, as outlined in the Communications Management Plan. It involves providing stakeholders with the right information, at the right time, in the right format, through the right channel.
◆Types of Information Distributed
  • Status Reports — current state of project scope, schedule, cost, and quality
  • Progress Reports — what has been accomplished during a reporting period
  • Performance Reports — EVM data, variance analysis, forecasts
  • Meeting Minutes — decisions and action items from meetings
  • Change Requests & Approvals — formal change documentation
  • Risk Updates — new risks, risk status changes, mitigation actions
◆Methods & Channels of Distribution

Push Communication

Information is sent to recipients without them requesting it. Emails, reports, status updates, newsletters. Good for ensuring everyone has the information.

Pull Communication

Information is stored centrally and recipients access it when needed. Shared drives, project portals, wikis, knowledge bases.

Interactive Communication

Two-way real-time exchange. Meetings, video calls, phone calls, instant messaging. Best for complex discussions.

Formal vs. Informal

Formal: official reports, presentations, signed documents. Informal: team chats, corridor conversations, quick emails.

◆Example — Software Development Project
📋

Scenario: ABC Corp is developing a payroll software system with a 6-month timeline. Information Distribution Plan:
Weekly — Project status email to all team members & sponsor (push).
Bi-weekly — Video call with steering committee reviewing EVM metrics.
Monthly — Formal progress report to client with milestone completion status.
Ongoing — JIRA board accessible to all stakeholders for task-level visibility (pull).
Ad hoc — Risk alerts sent immediately when a new critical risk is identified.

◆Tools for Information Distribution
  • Email / MS Outlook — for formal communication and status reports
  • MS Teams / Slack — for real-time team collaboration
  • SharePoint / Confluence — centralised document repositories (pull)
  • JIRA / Monday.com — real-time task and project status visibility
  • Zoom / Google Meet — virtual meetings and presentations

Question 13 · CO2 · Understand (BT2)

How to Develop the Project Budget? Explain in Brief.

BudgetCost ManagementCO2
◆What is a Project Budget?
A Project Budget is the total estimated cost of the project, including all approved costs for completing the project scope. It is derived by aggregating estimated costs of individual activities and serves as the financial baseline against which actual spending is measured and controlled.
◆Steps to Develop a Project Budget
Step 1 — Resource Planning

Identify what resources are needed for the project — people (developers, testers, designers), equipment, software licenses, office space, and materials. The project schedule and WBS guide this step.

Step 2 — Cost Estimation

Estimate the cost of each resource required. Common estimation techniques include:

  • Analogous Estimating — using costs from similar past projects
  • Parametric Estimating — using statistical relationships (e.g., cost per function point)
  • Bottom-Up Estimating — estimating each task individually and rolling up
  • Three-Point Estimating — (Optimistic + Most Likely + Pessimistic) / 3
  • Expert Judgment — consulting experienced professionals
Step 3 — Cost Aggregation

Aggregate individual activity costs up through the WBS to arrive at the total project cost estimate. Costs are summed at each WBS level from tasks → work packages → control accounts → total project.

Step 4 — Reserve Analysis

Add contingency reserves (for identified risks) and management reserves (for unknown unknowns) to the base estimate. Typically 5-15% of the project estimate depending on uncertainty level.

Step 5 — Establish Cost Baseline

The approved budget (excluding management reserves) becomes the cost baseline — the authorised time-phased budget used for measuring, monitoring, and controlling project cost performance.

Question 14 · CO2 · Understand (BT2)

Explain Project Communication Plan in Brief.

Communication PlanCO2
◆What is a Communication Plan?
A Project Communication Plan is a document that describes how, when, and by whom project information will be administered, collected, and distributed to stakeholders throughout the project life cycle. It is part of the overall Project Management Plan and ensures that all stakeholders receive the right information at the right time.
◆Why is a Communication Plan Needed?

Studies show that project managers spend up to 90% of their time communicating. Poor communication is cited as one of the top causes of project failure. A formal communication plan ensures nothing falls through the cracks.

◆Key Components of a Communication Plan
1. Stakeholder Identification

List all stakeholders who need to receive project communications — their names, roles, and contact information. Different stakeholders need different types and levels of information.

2. Information Requirements

Define what information each stakeholder needs: project status, technical details, financial reports, risk updates, etc. Senior management may need high-level summaries; technical teams need detailed task information.

3. Communication Method

Specify the channel and format for each communication: email, meetings, reports, dashboards, phone calls. Consider stakeholder preferences and geographic distribution.

4. Frequency & Timing

Define how often communications will occur — daily stand-ups, weekly status reports, monthly steering committee meetings, ad-hoc for urgent issues.

5. Responsible Person (Sender)

Identify who is responsible for preparing and distributing each communication. Accountability ensures communications are not missed.

Question 15 · CO2 · Understand (BT2)

Explain How to Develop and Finalize a Project Schedule.

Schedule DevelopmentWBSCO2
◆Process of Developing a Project Schedule

Developing a project schedule is an iterative process involving several steps. It is one of the most important planning activities in project management.

Step 1 — Define Activities

Break down the project scope using the Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) into individual, manageable activities/tasks. Each work package in the WBS is decomposed into schedule activities.

Example: WBS work package "Develop Login Module" → Activities: Design UI, Code backend API, Write unit tests, Code review, Integration testing.
Step 2 — Sequence Activities

Identify and document the logical relationships (dependencies) between activities. Determine which activities must finish before others can start.

  • Mandatory Dependencies — inherent in the nature of work (cannot code before design is complete)
  • Discretionary Dependencies — best practices (peer review before delivery)
  • External Dependencies — outside the project (vendor delivery before integration)
Step 3 — Estimate Activity Durations

Estimate how long each activity will take using techniques such as:

  • Expert Judgment — based on team experience
  • Analogous Estimating — based on similar past activities
  • Three-Point Estimating (PERT) — optimistic, most likely, pessimistic
  • Planning Poker — Agile estimation technique
Step 4 — Assign Resources

Assign team members, equipment, and materials to each activity. Check for resource conflicts and over-allocation. Balance workloads using resource levelling techniques.

Step 5 — Develop the Schedule Network

Build the project network diagram (PDM or ADM) connecting all activities with their dependencies. Calculate the Critical Path using forward and backward pass calculations.

Step 6 — Apply Schedule Compression (if needed)

If the initial schedule exceeds the required deadline, apply schedule compression techniques:

  • Crashing — adding resources to critical path activities to reduce duration (increases cost)
  • Fast-tracking — performing activities in parallel that were originally sequential (increases risk)
Step 7 — Finalise and Baseline the Schedule

Present the schedule to stakeholders for review and approval. Once approved, the schedule is baselined — it becomes the official reference point for measuring progress. Any future changes require formal change control.

▸ Primavera P6 — used for large, complex projects

▸ JIRA — Agile sprint planning and backlog management

▸ Smartsheet / Monday.com — collaborative online scheduling

Examine the Common Sources of Risk in IT Projects and Explain Their Impact on Project Success.

Risk SourcesIT RiskCO3BT3
◆What is Project Risk?
A project risk is an uncertain event or condition that, if it occurs, has a positive or negative effect on one or more project objectives such as scope, schedule, cost, or quality. In IT projects, risks are especially prevalent due to the complexity, novelty, and pace of technological change.
◆Common Sources of Risk in IT Projects
1. Requirement Risks

Incomplete, unclear, or changing requirements are the most common source of IT project risk. When requirements are not well-defined, developers build the wrong system, leading to costly rework.

⚠

Impact: Scope creep, rework, budget overrun, schedule delays, customer dissatisfaction.

2. Technology Risks

Using new, unproven, or rapidly changing technologies introduces significant uncertainty. Technical feasibility issues, lack of expertise, and integration challenges are common.

⚠

Impact: Integration failures, performance issues, security vulnerabilities, project failure.

3. Resource Risks

Loss of key personnel (key person dependency), skill gaps in the team, unavailability of resources, and high team turnover are major risks in IT projects where specific expertise is critical.

⚠

Impact: Schedule delays, knowledge loss, quality reduction, increased cost to hire replacements.

4. Schedule Risks

Unrealistic timelines, poor estimation, and dependencies on external parties (vendors, regulatory approvals) can cause schedule risks. IT projects frequently underestimate complexity.

⚠

Impact: Late delivery, rushed testing, reduced quality, stakeholder loss of confidence.

5. Budget / Cost Risks

Poor cost estimation, scope creep, and unexpected expenses (emergency hardware, additional licenses, overtime) lead to budget overruns. IT projects often face hidden costs.

⚠

Impact: Project cancellation, reduced scope, compromised quality, financial losses.

6. Security & Cybersecurity Risks

Data breaches, hacking, ransomware, and compliance violations are increasing risks for IT projects. Software with vulnerabilities can expose organisations to legal and financial liability.

⚠

Impact: Data loss, regulatory fines, reputational damage, project shutdown.

7. Vendor / Third-Party Risks

Dependency on external vendors for critical components, APIs, or services introduces risk. Vendor failure, price changes, or contract disputes can halt the project.

⚠

Impact: Integration delays, cost increases, project dependency bottlenecks.

8. Organisational / Political Risks

Lack of management support, organisational resistance to change, power struggles, and competing priorities are often underestimated risks that can sink an IT project.

⚠

Impact: Project cancellation, lack of resources, poor adoption of delivered solution.

Explain How Risk Management Planning Helps in Managing Risks in a Software Project.

Risk ManagementPlanningCO3BT3
◆What is Risk Management Planning?
Risk Management Planning is the process of deciding how to approach, plan, and execute risk management activities for a project. It results in a Risk Management Plan — a document that describes how risk processes will be structured and performed throughout the project lifecycle.
◆Risk Management Process (6 Steps)
Step 1 — Plan Risk Management

Define the methodology, roles, budget, timing, and categories for all risk management activities. Create the Risk Management Plan document. This ensures everyone agrees on how risks will be handled.

Step 2 — Identify Risks

Systematically identify all possible risks that could affect the project. Techniques used:

  • Brainstorming — team sessions to identify potential risks
  • Delphi Technique — anonymous expert consensus building
  • SWOT Analysis — Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats
  • Checklist Analysis — reviewing risks from past similar projects
  • Risk Register — maintained document listing all identified risks
Step 3 — Perform Qualitative Risk Analysis

Prioritise risks by assessing the probability of occurrence and potential impact (on a scale of Low/Medium/High). Use a Probability-Impact Matrix to categorise risks and determine which require the most attention.

Probability \ ImpactLowMediumHigh
HighMediumHighCritical
MediumLowMediumHigh
LowNegligibleLowMedium
Step 4 — Perform Quantitative Risk Analysis

For high-priority risks, perform numerical analysis to quantify their potential impact on project objectives. Techniques:

  • Expected Monetary Value (EMV) = Probability × Impact ($). Used in decision trees.
  • Monte Carlo Simulation — runs thousands of project simulations to model uncertainty
  • Sensitivity Analysis — determines which risks have the greatest overall impact
Step 5 — Plan Risk Responses

Develop strategies to address each risk. For negative risks (threats):

  • Avoid — eliminate the risk entirely (change the plan to avoid it)
  • Transfer — shift the risk to a third party (insurance, outsourcing)
  • Mitigate — reduce the probability or impact (more testing, prototyping)
  • Accept — acknowledge the risk and prepare a contingency plan

For positive risks (opportunities):

  • Exploit — ensure the opportunity definitely happens
  • Enhance — increase the probability of the opportunity
  • Share — partner with another party to capture the opportunity
Step 6 — Monitor and Control Risks

Track identified risks, identify new risks, evaluate risk process effectiveness, and execute contingency plans as needed throughout the project lifecycle. Risk audits and risk reassessments are conducted regularly.

◆How Risk Management Planning Helps
Benefits of Risk Management Planning

▸ Proactive — identifies problems before they occur, not after

▸ Reduces Surprises — fewer unexpected crises derail the project

▸ Better Decision Making — data-driven risk information supports informed choices

▸ Stakeholder Confidence — stakeholders trust a team that has anticipated risks

▸ Cost Savings — preventing a risk is almost always cheaper than recovering from it

▸ Improved Outcomes — projects with formal risk management have higher success rates

💡

Example: In a software migration project, risk management planning identified the risk of "data loss during migration" (High probability, Critical impact). Response: Mitigation — full backup before migration, rollback plan, dry-run migration on test data. Result: Migration completed successfully with zero data loss.
